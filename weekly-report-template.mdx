## Summary

**Three sentences: what happened, the one thing worth knowing, and what you're doing about it.**

_E.g. Revenue landed at \$103,000 against a \$100,000 weekly target, driven by Meta running above its normal efficiency range. Google Search spend paced 15% under budget because of a bid strategy change that took two days to recover from. We've adjusted bids back and expect Google to catch up by Friday._

| _E.g. Total marketing spend_ | _E.g. \$61,400_ | _E.g. \$60,000_ | _E.g. 102%_ | _E.g. Slightly over_ |
| \[add KPI\] |  |  |  |  |

<Accordion title="Concept review: Pacing">
  Compare against the target and the normal range, not against last week. Week-over-week movement on weekly data is mostly noise, a single large order, a day-of-week effect, one campaign's delivery pattern, and reacting to it teaches the report's readers to expect drama that isn't real. See [report writing concepts](/report-writing-concepts) for why cadence changes what a report should compare against.
</Accordion>

## Exceptions

**Only metrics genuinely outside their normal range. Leave this empty in a normal week.**

| Metric | Value | Normal range | What happened | Doing what |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| _E.g. Meta ROAS_ | _E.g. 4.8x_ | _E.g. 3.2 to 3.8x_ | _E.g. New creative outperforming; small audience, may not hold_ | _E.g. Watching before scaling budget_ |
| \[add exception\] |  |  |  |  |

<Accordion title="Concept review: Exceptions">
  This is the section that actually gets read. A metric moving inside its normal range is noise, and the right response is silence, not an invented explanation. Every exception needs a real cause or an honest "unknown, investigating"; a guess presented as a finding is worse than admitting you don't know yet.
</Accordion>
