---
title: "Campaign brief process"
---

A marketing campaign is a set of coordinated activities, across channels, creative, and people, that work toward one business goal. Campaigns can be small or large and often involve multiple stakeholders, teams, and deliverables, which makes them easy to run without a single source of truth unless one is deliberately set up.

The [**marketing campaign brief**](/marketing-campaign-brief) is that source of truth. It is more than a document with details in it: it is the portal to the campaign. From the brief, anyone should be able to reach every other part of the campaign, its customer profiles, creative briefs, copy briefs, finished assets, budget, timeline, and reporting, without having to ask around for links.

<Info>
  This process uses the [**marketing campaign brief**](/marketing-campaign-brief) workbook. Rebuild it inside a project management tool (Notion, Airtable, Monday, ClickUp, or similar) so tasks and progress can be tracked alongside the strategy.
</Info>

## What you'll end up with

One living document that points at everything else the campaign touches:

- A completed brief that records the campaign name, owner, objective, [customer profiles](/customer-profile-brief), messaging, channels, and KPIs.
- Links from that brief to every [creative brief](/creative-brief) and copy brief the campaign generated, and to the finished copy and creative once they land.
- A budget, a timeline, live reporting, and, once the campaign ends, a post-mortem, all reachable from the same page.

The brief is not fully done until the campaign is: it gets filled in before launch, updated while the campaign runs, and closed out with a post-mortem after it ends.

## Before you start

Confirm these before you open a new brief:

- You have the strategic inputs: a business goal, a sense of which [customer profiles](/customer-profile-library-overview) the campaign targets, and a rough channel mix.
- You have a project management tool to link the brief to, so tasks and progress can be tracked separately from the strategy.
- Your [customer profile library](/customer-profile-library-overview) and [campaign library](/campaign-library-overview) exist, so the brief can link to profiles and file itself for later reference instead of describing things ad hoc.

## The process at a glance

<Steps>
  <Step title="Set the foundation">
    Fill in the campaign name, owner, stakeholders, and objective.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Define the strategy">
    Link the target [customer profiles](/customer-profile-brief), write the key messaging and value proposition, choose the channels, and set [KPI](/kpi-definitions) targets.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Link the plumbing">
    Connect the project management board, [creative briefs](/creative-brief), copy briefs, reference documents, timeline, and budget.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Build and run">
    Add finished copy and creative as they land, keep notes current, and point live reporting dashboards at the brief.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Close the loop">
    Link the post-mortem, confirm KPI actuals against targets, and mark the brief complete.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## 1. Set the foundation

Give the campaign a distinctive, repeatable name and name a single owner accountable for it end to end. List other stakeholders and what each one owns, so the brief answers "who do I ask about the ads?" without a round of messages. Then state the objective in plain language: the business or marketing goal the campaign exists to move.

## 2. Define the strategy

Link every [customer profile](/customer-profile-library-overview) the campaign targets from the shared library rather than redescribing it in the brief. Write the core message and value proposition once, so every downstream creative and copy brief ladders up to the same idea. List the channels the campaign runs on and why each one fits, and set [KPI](/kpi-definitions) targets before launch, so there is a fixed bar to measure against later.

## 3. Link the plumbing

Connect the campaign brief to the project management board where day-to-day tasks live, and index every [creative brief](/creative-brief) and copy brief the campaign generates as they're created. Add the budget, broken into line items, and a timeline with key milestones including a mid-campaign review point. Link reference documents (product facts, brand guidelines, legal or compliance requirements) that the campaign's assets have to stay consistent with.

## 4. Build and run

As copy and creative are approved, link the finished versions into the brief so there's always one current, approved asset to point to. Keep a running notes section for meeting notes and decisions, so the reasoning behind a choice is retrievable later instead of lost in a chat thread. Point live reporting dashboards at the brief so the owner can spot a problem, or an opportunity, while there's still time to act on it.

<Warning>
  An empty finished-copy or finished-creative list on a campaign that's supposedly live is a signal something hasn't shipped yet. Treat a stale brief as a status problem to chase down, not a paperwork gap to ignore.
</Warning>

## 5. Close the loop

When the campaign ends, write the post-mortem: what the results were against the KPI targets, what worked, what didn't, why, and what to carry into the next campaign. Link it from the brief. This is what formally marks the brief, and the campaign, complete.

## Exceptions

- **The campaign is small and single-channel.** It still gets a brief, but conditional sections like competitive context or a formal approval step may not apply. Keep the core: name, owner, objective, customer profiles, and KPIs.
- **The campaign is cancelled mid-flight.** File the brief with a short note on why it stopped rather than a full post-mortem.
