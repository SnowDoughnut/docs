Run a piece of creative work from first brief to delivered asset. Copy the brief, fill in the strategy, file the completed brief in the library, hand it to a creator, review the work through defined feedback rounds, and link the finished creative back to the brief.

<Info>
  Each step uses one of these documents: the [**creative brief**](/creative-brief), the [**creative brief concepts**](/creative) document, the [**creative brief library**](/creative-briefs), and your [**brand system**](/brand-system-overview) documents ([brand and voice workbook](/untitled-page), [brand assets](/brand-assets), [positioning](/positioning), [messaging](/messaging), [logo usage](/logo-usage), [visual identity](/visual-identity), and [brand guidelines](/brand-guidelines)).
</Info>

## What you'll end up with

Two things that point at each other:

- A completed brief, filed in the [**creative brief library**](/creative-briefs), that records the strategy: the problem, audience, message, deliverables, and guardrails.
- A delivered creative asset, linked from the brief, that the creator produced against that strategy.

One person, the [**owner**](/glossary), is responsible for the brief end to end. The owner copies the brief, fills in the strategy, files it, hands it off, consolidates feedback, approves the work, and links the finished asset back. A second person, the [**creator**](/glossary), produces the work: the designer, art director, photographer, videographer, copywriter, or other maker who turns the brief into the asset. Others come in at set points: whoever approves the brief when sign-off is needed, and whoever reads the brief later to see what was decided.

## Before you start

Confirm these before you copy a new brief:

- Your team has agreed on a creative brief template. If not, set that up first in the [**creative brief**](/creative-brief).
- You have the strategic inputs: a problem to solve, an audience, and a sense of what the work must communicate.
- Your [**brand system**](/brand-system-overview) documents exist and are current, so the brief can link to them instead of restating brand rules.
- Your team has a [**creative brief library**](/creative-briefs) where completed briefs are filed. If not, set that up first.

## 1. Copy the brief

Make a fresh copy of the **creative brief** for this piece of work. Every assignment gets its own brief.

Don't reuse an old brief or start from a blank note. The standard brief already holds the sections you need, in the right order: the strategic core, the deliverables, the guardrails, and the supporting material.

## 2. Fill in the strategic core

Work down the brief. Record why the work is needed, who it's for, and what it must communicate:

- **Problem or opportunity:** the real situation the work responds to, stated in the audience's terms.
- **Objective:** the business goal, the communication objective, and the response you want from the audience.
- **Audience and insight:** who the primary audience is and the one thing about how they think that should steer the work.
- **Message and proof:** the single most important message, and the reasons the audience should believe it.
- **Deliverables:** exactly what must be produced, with format, dimensions, quantity, and deadline for each.
- **Guardrails:** what must appear and what must never appear.

See the **creative brief concepts** document for how to separate a problem from an objective, how to write an audience insight, and how to land on a single message.

## 3. Link the brand system and supporting material

Point the brief at the brand documents instead of retyping their rules. In the brief's supporting-material section, add links to the **brand system**: the **brand and voice workbook**, **brand assets**, **positioning**, **messaging**, **logo usage**, **visual identity**, and **brand guidelines**. Next to each link, note the part that applies to this job, so the creator isn't left reading a whole brand guide to find one rule.

Attach anything else the creator needs: approved product facts, the claims they may and may not make, existing copy, references, or prior performance data.

## 4. Get the brief approved, if the job needs it

Whether a brief needs sign-off before work starts depends on the job. Higher-stakes work, such as anything with significant spend, external talent, legal exposure, or senior visibility, is worth approving at the brief stage, because a wrong direction is far cheaper to fix on the brief than on a finished asset. A small internal adaptation may not need this step at all.

If the job needs sign-off, have the approver read the brief and confirm the direction before any creative begins. Record that the brief was approved, and by whom.

## 5. File the completed brief in the library

Save the finished brief to the **creative brief library** before work begins. Every completed brief goes there, whether or not it needed formal approval.

Filing the brief before the work starts, rather than after, means the strategy is on record from the moment anyone begins producing, and anyone who needs to see what was decided can find it while the work is still live.

## 6. Hand the brief to the creator

Give the **creator** the filed brief and confirm they have everything the deliverables call for: the brand links, the supporting material, and clear formats and deadlines. The creator produces the work; the brief is the strategy they build against.

A brief defines the problem and the boundaries and leaves room for the creator to solve it. Hand over the strategy and the guardrails, not a pre-written solution.

## 7. Run feedback rounds

Review each draft against the brief, not against personal preference. The brief's message, desired audience response, and success criteria are the standard the work is measured against.

- **Consolidate feedback.** Gather everyone's notes into one clear set per round, from a single point of contact, so the creator isn't chasing scattered or conflicting comments.
- **Tie each note to the brief.** A change is easier to act on when it points to the strategy: "this doesn't land the single most important message," not "I don't love it."
- **Track the rounds.** Work to the number of revision rounds the brief set. If the work needs more, that's a signal to check whether the brief itself was clear.

<Warning>
  Keep feedback tied to the brief. Notes that contradict the agreed strategy, or that move the goal after work has started, are the most common cause of a piece slipping late. If the strategy genuinely needs to change, update the brief first, then brief the change.
</Warning>

## 8. Approve the final work

Check the delivered asset against the success criteria the brief set in advance:

- **Creative acceptance:** on-brand, legible, complete, correctly representing the product, and delivered in every required format.
- **Everything on the guardrails list** is present, and nothing on the "must not include" list is.

Once the asset meets the criteria, approve it.

## 9. Link the finished creative back to the brief

Paste the link to the delivered asset into the brief, in the field for the finished creative. The brief now points to the work it produced, so anyone who opens the brief can reach the final asset, and anyone who finds the asset can trace it back to the strategy behind it.

## Exceptions

- **The job is a small internal adaptation.** A minor change to an existing, already-approved asset may not need the approval step or a full strategic core. It still gets a brief and still goes in the **creative brief library**, so there's a record of what changed and why.
- **The work is killed mid-flight.** If the assignment is cancelled before delivery, file the brief with a short note on why it stopped. There's no finished creative to link.
