## Core sections

### 1. Voice vs. tone

**In a sentence or two, describe how our voice stays the same no matter who we're talking to:**

_E.g. We're direct, warm, and confident in every piece of copy, whether it's a homepage headline or a one-line error message._

**How does our tone shift depending on the moment (e.g., talking to a close customer vs. someone who needs help)?**

_E.g. In a launch announcement we're upbeat and energetic. In an error message or support reply we slow down, stay calm, and focus on getting the reader unstuck._

<Accordion title="Concept review: Voice vs. tone">
  Voice and tone get used interchangeably, but they answer different questions. Voice answers "who is speaking?" and stays fixed. Tone answers "what's the emotional register right now?" and moves with the situation.

  The same person can be excited at a party and quiet at a funeral without having two personalities. That's the relationship between voice and tone: a brand can sound very different in an outage notice than in a product launch without becoming a different brand, because the underlying personality never actually changes, only its volume and warmth do.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Concept review: Voice traits">
  A trait like "friendly" or "bold" doesn't tell a writer much on its own, since almost any line of copy could be called friendly by someone. The contrast is what turns a trait into something usable: "bold, not reckless" tells a writer not just what to reach for, but where the trait stops.

### 5. Core style rules

Check off the defaults you're keeping, uncheck and delete any that don't fit, and add brand-specific rules at the end.

- Use active voice.
- Use contractions where they sound natural.
- Write in plain language.
- Keep sentences short when clarity matters.
- Use "you" when speaking directly to the reader.
- Use "we" when the brand is taking action or responsibility.
- Avoid jargon unless the audience expects it.
- Avoid vague hype.
- Be specific instead of sounding impressive.
- Use examples when a claim needs proof.
- Lead with the point.
- Make CTAs clear and action-oriented.
- Never use em dashes. Use a period, comma, colon, or parentheses instead.
- \[add brand-specific rule\]

<Accordion title="Concept review: Core style rules">
  These are mechanical, not philosophical: sentence length, contractions, active voice. They matter only in service of whatever voice was defined above, not as standalone principles.

  That's also why they're not universal. A brand writing for engineers might keep jargon a general-audience brand would cut, on purpose, because the jargon signals credibility to that specific reader rather than confusing them. "Avoid jargon" is downstream of a decision about the audience, not a rule that applies everywhere equally.
</Accordion>

### B. Point of view and pronoun rules

<Tip>
  Optional: include if copy includes product screens, transactional emails, onboarding, notifications, or support content
</Tip>

**When do you use "you"? When do you use "we"? Is first-person singular ("I") ever used, and by whom (mascot, founder, assistant)?**

_E.g. We use "you" when guiding the reader through the product, as in "You can assign tasks to teammates." We use "we" when Dough Labs is taking responsibility, as in "We fixed the bug you reported." We never use "I" since Dough Labs has no mascot or single named speaker._

<Accordion title="Concept review: Point of view and pronoun rules">
  A reader moving through a product, an email, a notification, is implicitly asking "who is talking to me right now?" every time the pronoun shifts. If "we" becomes "the team" becomes "you can also..." with no pattern, that question never quite gets answered clearly.

  Nothing here is individually wrong, but the accumulated effect is a reader who can't quite place who's speaking. Fixing the pronoun pattern is a small, cheap way to remove that friction.
</Accordion>

### C. Humor and wit

<Tip>
  Optional: include if the brand has any level of playfulness
</Tip>

**Where and how do you use humor? Where is it off-limits (e.g., errors, health, money, frustration)?**

_E.g. Light humor is fine in social posts and release notes, for example: "Bug squashed. It didn't stand a chance." Humor is off-limits in error messages, billing communications, and support replies about a cancellation._

<Accordion title="Concept review: Humor and wit">
  Brands land in very different places on humor, and both extremes can work when they're deliberate. Some keep humor entirely out of error messages, since a joke when something's broken can read as not taking the problem seriously. Others build humor into exactly those moments and it lands, because the humor is core to who they are rather than a mismatch with the situation.

  What doesn't work is humor that shows up inconsistently: present in some error messages, absent in others, for no reason a reader could ever guess.
</Accordion>

### D. Inclusive and global-audience language

<Tip>
  Optional: include if the brand writes for a broad or international audience
</Tip>

**What idioms, references, or assumptions should you avoid? Any specific inclusive-language notes for your audience?**

_E.g. Avoid idioms like "hit it out of the park," since they don't translate well. Use "team" instead of "guys." Avoid time references like "end of day" without naming a time zone._

<Accordion title="Concept review: Inclusive and global-audience language">
  An idiom or reference that reads as natural to one reader can read as confusing, or quietly exclude another. This section is closer to a risk ledger than a ban list.

  It's where a brand decides how much of that risk it's willing to accept, and for how much of its actual audience, rather than pretending the risk isn't there.
</Accordion>

### E. Handling bad news, apologies, and sensitive topics

<Tip>
  Optional: include if the brand communicates about errors, outages, pricing changes, delays, or other sensitive topics
</Tip>

**How do you acknowledge issues and apologize? What do you avoid (e.g., cheerful language, stiff legal language)?**

_E.g. State what happened, apologize once, then explain the fix. Example: "Dough Labs was down for 40 minutes today because of a database issue. Service is restored, and we're adding monitoring to catch this earlier next time." Avoid cheerful language and avoid stiff legal phrasing unless legally required._

<Accordion title="Concept review: Handling bad news, apologies, and sensitive topics">
  Readers tend to remember how a brand communicated bad news longer than they remember most other copy. That's what raises the stakes on this section compared to the others.

  It doesn't mean a brand's usual personality has to disappear here entirely. Some brands keep more of it than others in these moments. It does mean the choice deserves more deliberation than an ordinary product update would get.
</Accordion>

### F. Content-type-specific principles

<Tip>
  Optional: include if multiple teams write for the same brand
</Tip>

**Marketing copy**

_E.g. Lead with what the reader can now do, not the feature name._

**Product copy**

_E.g. Every button label describes the action, not the feature. Use "Add task," not "Task Creator."_

**Support copy**

_E.g. Always name the specific problem before the fix. Use "Your export failed because the file was too large," not "There was an error."_

<Accordion title="Concept review: Content-type-specific principles">
  Marketing copy is trying to persuade. Product copy is trying to instruct. Support copy is trying to resolve. Those are different jobs, even inside the same brand voice, and copy optimized purely for one job can undercut another. A persuasive headline pattern in a support reply can read as evasive, for instance, right when a reader wants a straight answer.

  Naming each team's job here is what keeps the differences between them intentional instead of accidental.
</Accordion>

### H. Brand mascot or character voice

<Tip>
  Optional: include if the brand has a mascot, avatar, founder voice, assistant, or recurring character
</Tip>

**Does the character speak? When does it appear? What would it never say?**

_E.g. Dough Labs has no mascot or character voice. All copy speaks as the brand itself, using "we," not as a persona._

<Accordion title="Concept review: Brand mascot or character voice">
  An undefined character drifts. Different writers imagine slightly different versions of what it would say, and over enough copy those versions pull apart into something inconsistent.

  This section only matters if a character actually exists. Most brands don't have one, and "no mascot" is a complete, valid answer here, not a gap to fill in.
</Accordion>
