## Core sections

### 1. Voice vs. tone

**In a sentence or two, describe how our voice stays the same no matter who we're talking to:**

_E.g. We're direct, warm, and confident in every piece of copy, whether it's a homepage headline or a one-line error message._

**How does our tone shift depending on the moment (e.g., talking to a close customer vs. someone who needs help)?**

_E.g. In a launch announcement we're upbeat and energetic. In an error message or support reply we slow down, stay calm, and focus on getting the reader unstuck._

### 5. Core style rules

Check off the defaults you're keeping, uncheck and delete any that don't fit, and add brand-specific rules at the end.

- Use active voice.
- Use contractions where they sound natural.
- Write in plain language.
- Keep sentences short when clarity matters.
- Use "you" when speaking directly to the reader.
- Use "we" when the brand is taking action or responsibility.
- Avoid jargon unless the audience expects it.
- Avoid vague hype.
- Be specific instead of sounding impressive.
- Use examples when a claim needs proof.
- Lead with the point.
- Make CTAs clear and action-oriented.
- Never use em dashes. Use a period, comma, colon, or parentheses instead.

### B. Point of view and pronoun rules

_Optional: include if copy includes product screens, transactional emails, onboarding, notifications, or support content_

**When do you use "you"? When do you use "we"? Is first-person singular ("I") ever used, and by whom (mascot, founder, assistant)?**

_We use "you" when guiding the reader through the product, as in "You can assign tasks to teammates." We use "we" when Northlane is taking responsibility, as in "We fixed the bug you reported." We never use "I" since Northlane has no mascot or single named speaker._

### C. Humor and wit

_Optional: include if the brand has any level of playfulness_

**Where and how do you use humor? Where is it off-limits (e.g., errors, health, money, frustration)?**

_Light humor is fine in social posts and release notes, for example: "Bug squashed. It didn't stand a chance." Humor is off-limits in error messages, billing communications, and support replies about a cancellation._

### D. Inclusive and global-audience language

_Optional: include if the brand writes for a broad or international audience_

**What idioms, references, or assumptions should you avoid? Any specific inclusive-language notes for your audience?**

_Avoid idioms like "hit it out of the park," since they don't translate well. Use "team" instead of "guys." Avoid time references like "end of day" without naming a time zone._

### E. Handling bad news, apologies, and sensitive topics

_Optional: include if the brand communicates about errors, outages, pricing changes, delays, or other sensitive topics_

**How do you acknowledge issues and apologize? What do you avoid (e.g., cheerful language, stiff legal language)?**

_State what happened, apologize once, then explain the fix. Example: "Northlane was down for 40 minutes today because of a database issue. Service is restored, and we're adding monitoring to catch this earlier next time." Avoid cheerful language and avoid stiff legal phrasing unless legally required._

### F. Content-type-specific principles

_Optional: include if multiple teams write for the same brand_

**Marketing copy**

_Lead with what the reader can now do, not the feature name._

**Product copy**

_Every button label describes the action, not the feature. Use "Add task," not "Task Creator."_

**Support copy**

_Always name the specific problem before the fix. Use "Your export failed because the file was too large," not "There was an error."_
