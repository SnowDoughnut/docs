The [Brand Voice & Tone Workbook ](/untitled-page)defines how our brand sounds, how our tone changes by context, and how we turn that into clear, consistent marketing copy. The goal is not to make every piece of copy sound identical. The goal is to make everything sound like it came from the same brand.

## How to use the [Brand Voice & Tone Workbook](/untitled-page)

1. Start with the core sections. These are the foundation of our brand voice.
2. Use the optional sections only where they apply to our channels, audience, product, and personality.
3. Keep it example-heavy. A real line of copy is more useful than a vague adjective.
4. Use this as a decision-making tool. When copy feels off, this document should help explain why and how to fix it.

# Core sections

## 1. Voice vs. tone

Voice is who we are. It stays consistent.

Tone is how our voice adapts to the reader's context, emotional state, and relationship with us. It changes depending on the situation.

A useful way to think about it:

> We have the same voice whether we are talking to a close friend, a new customer, or someone who needs help. But our tone changes depending on the moment.

This distinction matters because our brand should feel consistent without sounding robotic. A homepage headline, support reply, error message, and launch campaign should all sound like us, but they should not all use the same level of energy, warmth, or personality.

## 2. Voice traits

Our voice is built around 3–5 core traits.

Each trait needs to be specific enough to guide actual writing. Generic traits like "friendly," "innovative," or "trustworthy" are not useful on their own. They only become useful when we define what they mean for us and what they are not.

Each voice trait should include:

- A short name
- A clear description
- A contrast that explains what the trait is not
- A bad example
- A better example

The contrast is important. It prevents the trait from becoming generic or being stretched too far.

For example:

- Bold, not reckless
- Helpful, not hand-holding
- Confident, not arrogant
- Warm, not overly casual
- Direct, not blunt

Use this structure for each trait:

### \[Trait Name\]

Description:

\[What this trait means for our brand specifically.\]

Not:

\[What this trait could be mistaken for, and what we want to avoid.\]

Don't:

\[Example of this trait done poorly.\]

Do:

\[Example of this trait done well.\]

## 3. Tone principles

Tone is how we adapt our voice to the moment.

Before writing, we should ask:

- What does the reader need right now?
- What is the reader likely thinking or feeling?
- How familiar are they with us?
- Are they excited, confused, skeptical, annoyed, curious, or ready to act?
- Is this a high-stakes or low-stakes moment?

Our tone should flex based on the context, but it should never abandon our voice.

For example:

- In a campaign, we can be more energetic and memorable.
- In a pricing page, we should be clear, direct, and trust-building.
- In an error message, we should be calm, useful, and human.
- In a support reply, we should be respectful, specific, and focused on resolution.
- In a social post, we can use more personality, but still need to sound like us.

Tone should never be random. It should respond to the reader's situation.

## 4. Voice and tone in practice

This is the most important section for day-to-day writing.

Include before-and-after examples that show what off-brand copy looks like and how we would rewrite it.

Use a range of examples across our actual channels, such as:

- Homepage headline
- Product or service description
- Paid ad
- Email subject line
- Social post
- Error message
- Support reply
- CTA
- Launch announcement
- Pricing or offer copy

Example format:

| Situation | Off-brand | On-brand |

| ----------------- | ------------------------------ | ------------- |

| Homepage headline | \[Generic or off-brand version\] | \[Our version\] |

| Paid ad | \[Generic or off-brand version\] | \[Our version\] |

| Support reply | \[Generic or off-brand version\] | \[Our version\] |

This section should make the rules obvious without forcing people to interpret abstract voice traits.

## 5. Core style rules

These are the practical writing mechanics that help our voice show up consistently.

They should exist because of our brand voice, not because of random grammar preferences.

Examples:

- Use active voice.
- Use contractions where they sound natural.
- Write in plain language.
- Keep sentences short when clarity matters.
- Use "you" when speaking directly to the reader.
- Use "we" when the brand is taking action or responsibility.
- Avoid jargon unless the audience expects it.
- Avoid vague hype.
- Be specific instead of sounding impressive.
- Use examples when a claim needs proof.
- Lead with the point.
- Make CTAs clear and action-oriented.

This section should stay short. It is not a full grammar guide. It is a set of writing rules that help our brand sound like itself.

# Optional sections

## A. Tone-by-channel matrix

Use this if our brand shows up across different types of content, such as ads, landing pages, email, social, product UI, and support.

Different channels need different amounts of personality.

A support message should not sound like a paid ad. A campaign headline should not sound like a help article. A pricing page should not sound like a social caption.

Example structure:

| Channel | Tone | Personality Level | Notes |

| ---------- | ---------------------------------- | ----------------: | -------------------------------------- |

| Homepage | Clear, confident, memorable | Medium | Explain the value quickly. |

| Paid ads | Direct, punchy, benefit-led | High | Lead with the hook or offer. |

| Email | Helpful, focused, human | Medium | Match the reader's stage. |

| Social | Conversational, timely, expressive | High | More room for personality. |

| Support | Calm, useful, respectful | Low | Solve the issue first. |

| Product UI | Clear, simple, action-oriented | Low | Avoid cleverness when clarity matters. |

## B. Point of view and pronoun rules

Use this if our copy includes product screens, transactional emails, onboarding, notifications, or support content.

Define who is speaking and who we are speaking to.

Guidelines to decide:

- Use "you" when speaking directly to the reader.
- Use "we" when the brand is taking responsibility or explaining what we are doing.
- Avoid switching between "I," "we," and the brand name without a reason.
- Only use first-person singular if the brand has a clear assistant, mascot, founder, or individual speaker.
- Be careful with "our users" or "customers" when "you" would feel more direct.

The reader should always know who is talking and what action is being asked of them.

## C. Humor and wit

Use this if our brand has any level of playfulness.

Humor can make copy more memorable, but it should never get in the way of clarity or trust.

Rules:

- Humor is a seasoning, not the meal.
- Never make the reader the joke.
- Do not use humor when someone is frustrated, confused, losing money, dealing with a health issue, or facing a serious problem.
- Avoid jokes in error messages unless the issue is very low-stakes.
- Do not force wit into copy that needs to be clear.
- A simple line is usually better than a clever line that distracts from the message.

Humor should feel natural to the brand, not like a writer trying to prove they are funny.

## D. Inclusive and global-audience language

Use this if we write for a broad audience, operate across regions, localize content, or want our copy to be easier for more people to understand.

Guidelines:

- Avoid idioms that may not translate.
- Avoid culture-specific references unless they are intentional and relevant.
- Use plain language.
- Avoid stereotypes or assumptions about the reader.
- Use inclusive terminology.
- Avoid phrases with problematic origins where clearer alternatives exist.
- Write in a way that can be translated without losing the core meaning.

Good brand voice should not depend on inside jokes, regional slang, or references that only part of the audience understands.

## E. Handling bad news, apologies, and sensitive topics

Use this if our brand has to communicate about errors, outages, pricing changes, delays, financial topics, health topics, legal topics, or service issues.

Guidelines:

- Be direct.
- Acknowledge the issue.
- Apologize when we caused the problem.
- Do not over-apologize when we did not cause the issue.
- Say sorry once, sincerely, then move to the fix.
- Explain what happened if we can.
- Tell the reader what happens next.
- Avoid stiff legal language unless legally required.
- Do not use cheerful language to soften something serious.

Serious does not have to mean cold. Clear, calm, and human is usually better than formal and vague.

## F. Content-type-specific principles

Use this if multiple teams write for the same brand, such as marketing, product, support, sales, and social.

The same voice should carry across everything, but each type of writing needs its own rules.

For example:

### Marketing copy

Marketing copy should be clear, persuasive, and memorable. It should lead with the customer problem, outcome, offer, or reason to care.

Principles:

- Lead with the value.
- Make the benefit specific.
- Avoid empty hype.
- Make the next action obvious.
- Use personality to sharpen the message, not decorate it.

### Product copy

Product copy should help people understand, decide, and act.

Principles:

- Prioritize clarity over cleverness.
- Use familiar words.
- Keep labels short.
- Explain consequences before important actions.
- Help the reader recover when something goes wrong.

### Support copy

Support copy should make people feel heard and helped.

Principles:

- Be calm and specific.
- Avoid blame.
- Give the next step.
- Use empathy without sounding scripted.
- Resolve the issue before adding personality.

## G. Word list and terminology guide

Use this if we have product terms, category language, internal phrases, or banned words that writers need to use consistently.

Create a living table:

| Term | Use | Avoid | Why |

| --------------- | --------------- | ----------------- | -------- |

| \[Approved term\] | \[How to use it\] | \[Terms to avoid\] | \[Reason\] |

This should be separate from the main voice document so it can be updated often without changing the brand fundamentals.

Include:

- Approved product names
- Category terms
- Words we avoid
- Preferred spellings
- Competitor comparison language
- Legal or compliance-sensitive phrases
- Internal terms that should not appear in customer-facing copy

## H. Brand mascot or character voice

Use this if our brand has a mascot, avatar, founder voice, assistant, or recurring character.

Define what the character does and does not do.

Guidelines:

- The character should not create a second brand voice unless that is intentional.
- The character should support the brand, not replace it.
- Define when the character appears.
- Define whether the character speaks.
- Define what the character would never say.
- Keep the character consistent across channels.

This prevents a mascot or assistant from becoming an accidental second personality for the brand.

## I. Rationale and rollout

Use this if the voice guide needs to be adopted by multiple people or teams.

Explain why the guide exists.

For example:

- Our copy has become inconsistent across channels.
- Different teams are using different levels of formality.
- Our ads, website, emails, and support messages do not sound connected.
- We need a clearer system for reviewing copy.
- We want the brand to become more recognizable over time.

Then explain how the guide should be used:

- Onboard new writers with it.
- Use it during copy reviews.
- Add examples as new use cases come up.
- Revisit it when the brand strategy changes.
- Keep terminology and examples updated.

The goal is not to police every sentence. The goal is to make better copy easier to write.

# Quick reference: build order

1. Define voice vs. tone.
2. Choose 3–5 voice traits.
3. Add contrast for each trait.
4. Define how tone flexes by context.
5. Add before-and-after examples.
6. Add core style rules.
7. Layer in optional sections where needed.
